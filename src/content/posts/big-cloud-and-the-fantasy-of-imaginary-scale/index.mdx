<Lede>
There is a strange disease in software infrastructure conversations. A company has no
product in production yet. No real traffic. No mature deployment pipeline. No operational
team. No customer scale. No existing cloud investment. Sometimes not even a finished
backend. And then someone says the magic words.
</Lede>

<PullQuote>
But we should use AWS, because we want to scale.
</PullQuote>

This sentence has probably burned more startup engineering hours than bad ORM defaults.

And I get it. I really do. AWS sounds serious. AWS sounds enterprise. AWS sounds like the
responsible adult in the room. It gives people the feeling that the company is preparing
for success. But that is exactly the problem.

A lot of infrastructure decisions are not made from technical need. They are made from
**infrastructure aesthetics**. AWS feels like scale. Kubernetes feels like scale.
Multi-account cloud strategy feels like scale. Private subnets, IAM boundaries, Terraform
modules, service meshes, event buses, observability pipelines — all of this feels like
the architecture of a company that already won.

But most companies are not there. Most companies are still trying to discover whether
the product deserves to exist. And at that stage, choosing infrastructure because it
resembles the future version of your company is usually just a very expensive form of
cosplay.

<PullQuote>
A Formula 1 car is fast. It is also a terrible vehicle for grocery shopping.
</PullQuote>

## The cloud is not one thing anymore

For a long time, "cloud" basically meant AWS. Then Azure and Google Cloud became the
other default names in the room. But the world around them changed.

The choice is no longer the binary it used to be:

<Compare
  leftEyebrow="Old world"
  leftTitle="Buy servers, or use AWS"
  rightEyebrow="New world"
  rightTitle="A serious middle category"
>
  <div slot="left">
    For most of the 2010s the only respectable answers were *rack your own metal* or
    *go all-in on a hyperscaler*. Everything between those extremes was treated as a toy
    tier — fine for side projects, never for "real" software.
  </div>
  <div slot="right">
    Cloudflare, Fly.io, Render, Railway, Koyeb, Sevalla, DigitalOcean, Scaleway, and
    others are building opinionated infrastructure products with modern defaults. Some
    are PaaS-like, some edge-first, some microVM-based, some are self-service clouds with
    nicer ergonomics. None of them are toys.
  </div>
</Compare>

I like calling them **Cloud Tigers**. Not because they are bigger than the hyperscalers —
they are not. But because they are fast, focused, ambitious, and often much better
aligned with what small and medium software teams actually need:

- ship the product
- keep ops low
- avoid unnecessary bureaucracy
- scale when needed
- do not build an internal platform team cosplay

That last one is important. A lot of companies do not need a hyperscaler. They need a
path to production that does not punish them for being small.

## The hyperscaler argument is usually emotional

The argument usually sounds rational:

> We need AWS because we plan to scale.

But under the surface, it is often emotional. AWS feels serious. AWS feels enterprise.
AWS feels like the company is preparing for success. It creates the sensation that the
business is already on the path to becoming something huge.

This is the fetish part. The infrastructure choice becomes a symbol. AWS is treated less
like a tool and more like a maturity costume.

But infrastructure should not be chosen for the imaginary future version of the company
that has ten million users, a platform team, an SRE rotation, reserved instances,
multi-region strategy, compliance automation, internal developer portals, and a dedicated
Slack channel for IAM disasters.

It should be chosen for the company that exists *now*. And the company that exists now
probably needs:

- `git push` deploys
- preview environments
- managed Postgres
- background jobs
- object storage
- simple secrets
- logs that are not a scavenger hunt
- metrics that humans can read
- low-friction rollbacks
- a boring deployment story
- a bill that does not require FinOps divination

Not a two-hundred-service buffet where every service has its own pricing page, permission
model, deployment pattern, and footgun.

## DevOps is not supposed to be soul damage

There is also something people get wrong about DevOps. DevOps teams are supposed to be
**DevOps**, not just Ops with a cooler job title and YAML trauma.

Building an architecture stack can be fun. It can be neat. It can be creative
engineering. Designing deployment flows, observability, environment strategy, service
boundaries, database lifecycle, background processing, failure recovery — that is real
engineering work.

But managing hyperscaler bureaucracy is different.

:::important Soul-tax, not engineering

IAM archaeology is not product engineering. Fighting permission policies is not
architecture. Spending days understanding why one cloud service can talk to another only
when the correct ritual of roles, policies, trust relationships, regions, VPC settings,
security groups, and service-linked whatever has been performed is not innovation.

It eats your soul. And it costs money either way. Either you pay the cloud bill, or you
pay more salaries for people whose job is to survive the cloud bill. Sometimes both.

:::

Infrastructure cost is not only compute, storage, and bandwidth. It is also:

- learning curve
- deployment friction
- debugging time
- operational surface area
- security configuration burden
- billing unpredictability
- vendor-specific knowledge
- team cognitive load
- time not spent on product

A startup does not only run out of money. It runs out of attention. Every hour spent
debugging IAM permissions for a service the team barely understands is an hour not spent
improving onboarding, fixing product gaps, talking to users, reducing churn, building
core domain logic, or making the software actually valuable.

This is why "AWS is cheaper at scale" can be a misleading argument. At what scale? With
what team? With what workload? With what reserved capacity? With what operational
maturity? With how many engineers quietly becoming infrastructure maintainers?

A small team choosing AWS for theoretical future scale may be buying *negative velocity
today* in exchange for an *imaginary discount tomorrow*. That is bad finance and worse
engineering.

<KeyTakeaway label="The trap">
Pick infrastructure for the company that exists now, not the imaginary IPO version. The
hyperscaler tax is real, even when the cloud bill itself looks reasonable.
</KeyTakeaway>

<SectionDivider n="02" label="The Big Three" />

## AWS deserves respect — and skepticism

I respect AWS. No serious person should write about cloud infrastructure while
pretending AWS is incompetent. AWS created the default mental model for modern cloud. It
is massive, mature, battle-tested, and it can support workloads most companies will
never come close to having.

AWS is, in many ways, **the cloud**.

But this is also why AWS is dangerous for early teams. AWS is no longer just an
infrastructure product. It feels more like a *raw computing civilization*. It gives you
everything. But "everything" is not the same as a good product experience.

This is where the AWS fetish becomes obvious. There are entire companies that use AWS as
the underlying compute layer and then build *better infrastructure products on top of
it*. That is not random. That is a market signal. If AWS-native products were always the
best way to consume AWS primitives, half of this platform ecosystem would not need to
exist.

AWS has products for everything: containers, functions, queues, databases, object
storage, workflows, APIs, logs, identity, networking, deployment, secrets, monitoring,
and probably three different services for whatever word you just thought of. But the
experience often feels like walking through sedimentary layers of Amazon history.

Powerful? Yes. Elegant? Not usually.

ECS is a good example. It is simpler than Kubernetes, and for many AWS-native teams it
is a reasonable choice. But it is still proprietary orchestration complexity. It is
complex enough that you need to learn it seriously. And once I need to learn proprietary
orchestration anyway, I start asking:

<PullQuote>
Why am I learning this instead of using Kubernetes, Nomad, Fly Machines, or a higher-level
platform that removes most of this concern entirely?
</PullQuote>

This is the uncomfortable truth: you are not receiving the benefits of being Netflix.
You are receiving the homework of someone who wants to look like they might become
Netflix one day.

## Azure: the enterprise default nobody loves out loud

Azure is harder to talk about because Azure's power is not developer love. It is
distribution.

Azure wins because companies already live inside Microsoft. Identity, enterprise
contracts, procurement, compliance, Active Directory, Office, Teams, SharePoint, Power
BI, Dynamics, Windows Server history — Azure enters through the side door labeled:

> We already pay Microsoft.

That is a valid business advantage. Sometimes that is exactly the right answer.

But if you are a small team choosing Azure from scratch because you think it is the best
developer experience for a greenfield product, I have questions.

Azure often feels like infrastructure designed by a committee that was interrupted by
another committee. There are strong services. There are serious enterprise capabilities.
But the product experience is rarely the cleanest path from idea to production. Azure is
excellent when the organization context demands Azure. It is rarely the best answer when
the only requirement is:

> We need to ship fast and not drown.

## Google Cloud: the genius with an identity crisis

Google Cloud is the big cloud I find the most interesting, because you can see the taste
trying to escape.

GCP has good ideas. Cloud Run is one of the best mainstream container deployment
experiences. BigQuery is excellent. Google's Kubernetes heritage is real. Some parts of
the platform feel modern, clean, and developer-aware.

And then, suddenly, you are back inside big-cloud bureaucracy.

That is the strange thing about GCP. It often feels like two companies fighting each
other. One company wants to build an elegant developer platform. The other wants to be a
hyperscaler with all the enterprise knobs, IAM ceremony, product overlap, quota
weirdness, org policies, and documentation archaeology that come with that ambition.

Google Cloud should be the opinionated hyperscaler. It should be the one that says:

> We know the modern path. Here it is.

Instead, it sometimes feels undecided between being the infrastructure platform of the
future and being "AWS, but with Google colors." That is probably part of why Azure,
despite being much less exciting from a developer-taste perspective, is still ahead of
it. Azure has distribution. Azure has Microsoft gravity. Azure has enterprise lock-in as
a superpower. GCP has better taste in many places, but it does not always have a clear
enough stance.

## The Cloud Tigers

The more interesting infrastructure story is happening below the hyperscaler layer. Not
because the smaller players are universally better — they are not. They have trade-offs,
limits, outages, missing features, regional constraints, support differences, and
sometimes pricing surprises of their own.

But they usually start from a different question.

<Compare
  leftEyebrow="Hyperscaler"
  leftTitle="What primitive can we expose?"
  rightEyebrow="Cloud Tiger"
  rightTitle="What experience should the developer have?"
>
  <div slot="left">
    The catalogue is the product. Pricing pages multiply. Each service ships with its
    own permissions model, deployment pattern, and footgun. Composing them is the work.
  </div>
  <div slot="right">
    The developer journey is the product. Primitives are chosen, opinionated, sometimes
    fewer. The platform makes a real claim about how things should fit together.
  </div>
</Compare>

That changes everything.

## Cloudflare: pick a side

Cloudflare is probably the strangest and most interesting player in this entire
conversation. And I should be honest here: Cloudflare is not a neutral cloud. It is not
trying to give you the same mental model as AWS with a nicer dashboard. It has its own
concepts, its own runtime model, its own edge-first worldview, and its own collection of
primitives that can feel weird until they click.

Workers are not just tiny Node servers. Durable Objects are not Redis with a Cloudflare
sticker — they are closer to stateful actors that give you a coordination point for a
specific room, user, document, session, game, or workflow. R2 is not just S3 with orange
paint. D1 is not just "SQLite but cloud." The whole platform has its own physics.

And yes, sometimes the physics are strange. But this is *product-strange*, not
*bureaucracy-strange*. That distinction matters.

:::tip The shape of the weirdness

AWS weirdness often feels like paperwork. Cloudflare weirdness feels like design
philosophy. You may agree with the philosophy or not, but at least there is one.

:::

Cloudflare wants you to build in a specific way: globally distributed, edge-aware,
event-driven, latency-sensitive, with managed coordination primitives and a runtime that
does not look like the traditional "server in a region behind a load balancer" model.

That is why I jokingly call Cloudflare the *Apple of cloud infrastructure*. Not because
the branding is bad — Cloudflare has a strong brand. It is just not Apple-level "the
whole thing feels like one polished object" yet. The Apple comparison is more about the
*product posture*. Cloudflare says:

> Do it our way.

Which can be annoying. But the important difference is: their way is often good.
Sometimes extremely good. Sometimes better than the traditional cloud model by a lot.

This is what makes Cloudflare so interesting. It is proprietary. It is opinionated. It
has weird primitives. It can absolutely lock you into its worldview. But the worldview
is not random. It is coherent. It is modern. It is built around the idea that global
infrastructure should be accessible without assembling a private distributed systems
department.

That is rare. So yes, I am biased. People call me a Workers Maniac. I like when
infrastructure has *taste*. I like when a provider says, "Here is a better primitive,"
instead of giving me a thousand knobs and calling that flexibility.

But if you use Cloudflare, you need to accept the trade. You are not buying generic
cloud. You are buying into a worldview. For me, that is often worth it.

## Fly.io: freedom, control, and looking good while doing it

Fly.io is the conceptual one. I do not mean that in a shallow landing-page way. I mean
Fly has one of the best conceptual models in modern infrastructure:

<PullQuote>
Run real applications, inside Firecracker microVMs, close to users, with a strong global
networking layer.
</PullQuote>

That is just cool. Fly gives me something I care about a lot: **freedom with low ops**.

I do not want to be trapped inside a narrow platform where everything is magical until
the moment I need to do something slightly outside the expected path. But I also do not
want to spend my life assembling cloud primitives like IKEA furniture from hell.

Fly hits a very interesting middle point. It gives you machines. Real machine-like
things. Your app runs in a container. You get global placement. You get private
networking. You get enough control to feel like an adult, but not so much ceremony that
you need a platform team just to deploy a backend.

That is the sweet spot.

It is lower-level than Render or Railway. It is less "click here and forget." But it is
also far less bureaucratic than hyperscaler infrastructure. You can understand the
model. You can reason about it. You can build serious always-on services without
immediately falling into Kubernetes-shaped complexity.

It feels like infrastructure designed by people who still think computers are cool. That
matters more than people admit. Because when a platform has taste, your own architecture
tends to get better. When the primitives are clean, your system design has fewer excuses
to become ugly.

Fly is not perfect. No provider is. But conceptually, it is one of the strongest
alternatives to the hyperscaler default — a highly conceptual, badass, innovative model
for teams who want low ops, edge-friendly Firecracker microVMs, *and* to look good while
doing it.

## Railway, Render, Koyeb, Sevalla: Heroku was right

People forget how magical Heroku felt.

```sh
git push heroku main
```

That was not just a deployment command. It was a statement: developers should not need
to become infrastructure monks to ship software.

<PullQuote>
Heroku was right. The market just spent a decade rediscovering that.
</PullQuote>

After everyone collectively decided that YAML, Kubernetes, Terraform, sidecars, service
meshes, and cloud IAM would somehow make us happier — Railway, Render, Koyeb, Sevalla,
and similar platforms are part of the correction. They say:

- connect your repo
- configure your services
- deploy your app
- add a database
- add environment variables
- scale when needed
- move on

That is not childish. That is not unserious. That is exactly what many teams need.

### Railway

Railway leans hardest into DX. It is fast, friendly, template-driven, and almost
aggressively optimized for getting from idea to running system. It feels like a platform
built by people who understand that early software work is exploratory.

You do not always know what the architecture should be — you are discovering it. Railway
is very good for that phase. The risk is that convenience can blur boundaries. You need
to keep an eye on usage and structure. But that is a much better problem than spending
three weeks designing AWS networking for a product nobody has used yet.

Railway gives you momentum. And momentum is underrated.

### Render

Render is probably the cleanest "modern Heroku" mental model. Web services, workers,
cron jobs, private services, managed databases, Git deploys, preview environments — it
has the pieces most teams need without making every deployment feel like a cloud
certification exam.

Render is not as conceptually wild as Cloudflare or Fly. That is fine. Its value is
being boring in the right way. There is a category of product where I do not want
edge-native state coordination or microVM placement strategy. I just want a backend, a
worker, a database, logs, deploys, and sleep. Render is good for that.

:::warning Cold-start tax

Beware cold starts depending on tier and service shape, especially if you are expecting
always-warm behavior. As a platform model, Render is solid — just price the warmth you
actually need.

:::

### Koyeb

Koyeb is interesting because it feels like a more global, container-native,
serverless-flavored PaaS. It supports Git and Docker deployments, autoscaling,
scale-to-zero, services, and a clean flow for running apps without owning the
orchestration layer.

It sits in a nice position: more explicit runtime flexibility than some simple PaaS
offerings, but still much less operational burden than hyperscaler setups. Koyeb is the
kind of provider I would look at when I want modern app hosting, containers, global
deployment options, and a lighter operational model. Not revolutionary in the Cloudflare
or Fly sense, but very relevant.

### Sevalla

Sevalla is newer, from the Kinsta team, and it feels like a managed-hosting company
expanding into broader application infrastructure. That is not a bad thing.

Kinsta understands managed web operations. Sevalla seems to take that sensibility and
apply it to applications, databases, static sites, CDN integration, Git-based deploys,
Docker support, and building-block-style infrastructure. It does not radically change
the category. It is more like:

> Here is another clean, modern, high-DX platform from a team that understands hosting.

That is valid. Not every provider needs to reinvent computing. Sometimes the win is just
removing enough friction that the team keeps shipping.

<SectionDivider n="04" label="Self-Service Clouds" />

## DigitalOcean and Scaleway: self-service without enterprise theater

Not every team wants a PaaS. Sometimes you want a more self-service cloud. You want VMs,
managed databases, object storage, Kubernetes if necessary, networking, and normal
infrastructure primitives — but without the feeling that you accidentally joined a
Fortune 500 cloud migration program.

This is where DigitalOcean and Scaleway are very strong.

### DigitalOcean

DigitalOcean has always understood developers better than most big clouds. It is not
the newest or flashiest option, but it has a very clear value: familiar infrastructure,
lower ceremony, approachable product experience.

Droplets, managed databases, App Platform, Kubernetes, Spaces, load balancers — nothing
here is conceptually confusing. That is the point. DigitalOcean is good when you want
something closer to traditional cloud infrastructure but do not want hyperscaler
bureaucracy. It is the *give me servers and managed services without making me hate
computers* option. For many companies, that is more than enough.

### Scaleway

Scaleway is excellent. It deserves more attention than it gets, especially if you care
about European infrastructure, sovereignty, and avoiding the big three without falling
back to some tiny VPS provider with questionable durability.

Scaleway has a real cloud portfolio: compute, managed databases, Kubernetes, object
storage, serverless containers, serverless functions, AI infrastructure, networking, and
more. It is not just "cheap servers in Europe."

And the European angle matters. Data sovereignty is not only a buzzword anymore — it is
becoming a real architectural and procurement concern. For companies operating in
Europe, serving European customers, or wanting a provider outside the US hyperscaler
triangle, Scaleway is one of the most compelling options. It has enough primitives to
build serious systems, but it still feels more approachable than the big-cloud maze.

For self-service infrastructure with a European stance, Scaleway is one of the best
names in the conversation.

## The migration fear is overblown

Another common argument:

> If we start outside AWS, migrating later will be hard.

Yes. Migration is work. But early over-engineering is also work. Usually more work. And
unlike migration work, it happens *before* the product has proved it deserves the
complexity.

You can also make migration easier without starting on a hyperscaler:

- use containers
- keep state in boring databases
- avoid vendor-specific business logic
- keep queues abstracted behind small interfaces
- use S3-compatible object storage when possible
- keep infrastructure definitions readable
- avoid turning platform-specific features into your domain model
- separate application architecture from deployment architecture

A well-designed app on Fly, Render, Railway, Koyeb, DigitalOcean, Scaleway, or
Cloudflare can be migrated later if the business earns that problem. But a badly
designed app on AWS is not magically portable because the logo was enterprise-approved.

Starting on AWS does not prevent lock-in. It often *accelerates* it. IAM, Lambda, Step
Functions, DynamoDB, EventBridge, SQS, SNS, Cognito, API Gateway, CloudWatch, CDK, ECS,
EKS, Fargate — these are powerful services, but they are not neutral abstractions. They
shape your system. Sometimes that is fine. But do not pretend the hyperscaler path is
the anti-lock-in path. It is usually just lock-in with better enterprise procurement.

## When hyperscalers are the right answer

This article is not an argument that nobody should use AWS, GCP, or Azure. Hyperscalers
make sense when you actually need hyperscaler-shaped things:

- large enterprise contracts
- strict customer procurement requirements
- deep compliance programs
- existing cloud commitments
- complex private networking
- specialized managed services
- massive data platforms
- advanced IAM/org structures
- global multi-region enterprise workloads
- GPU/AI capacity at serious scale
- tight integration with Microsoft, Google, or Amazon ecosystems
- a team that already knows the platform well

AWS is a very good answer when the organization already has AWS expertise, AWS
commitments, AWS security controls, AWS landing zones, AWS observability, and AWS
operational muscle. Azure is a very good answer when the company is deeply
Microsoft-native. GCP is a very good answer when the workload fits its strengths: data,
analytics, Cloud Run, GKE, BigQuery, Pub/Sub, ML tooling, and Google's network.

<KeyTakeaway label="The real mistake">
The mistake is not choosing a hyperscaler. The mistake is choosing a hyperscaler because
it *feels* adult.
</KeyTakeaway>

## A better rule

Here is the rule I wish more teams followed: pick infrastructure for your **next 18
months**.

Not your imaginary IPO architecture. Not your "what if we become Uber?" architecture.
Not your "I watched three re:Invent talks and now everything is an event-driven
multi-account landing zone" architecture.

For many teams, that means:

- **Cloudflare** for edge-native, globally distributed, event-driven systems
- **Fly.io** for globally deployed containers and always-on services with great
  networking
- **Render, Railway, Koyeb, or Sevalla** for high-DX app hosting and managed services
- **DigitalOcean** for a clean self-service cloud with familiar primitives
- **Scaleway** for European cloud, sovereignty, and a serious alternative cloud portfolio
- **AWS, GCP, or Azure** when their specific strengths or organizational requirements
  are real

That is not anti-cloud. That is *pro-fit*.

## The point of infrastructure is not infrastructure

The point of infrastructure is to let the product exist.

Users do not care if your startup has a beautiful Terraform module layout. They do not
care if your ECS cluster is properly named. They do not care if your private subnets
look good in a diagram. They do not care if your IAM policies express the deep sadness
of least privilege.

They care that the product works. They care that it is fast enough, reliable enough,
secure enough, and improving fast enough.

For a young company, infrastructure should create *leverage*. If it creates *drag*, it
is the wrong infrastructure — even if it is "industry standard."

The hyperscalers are incredible. They are also often too much machine for the job. And
the funny thing is: choosing a smaller, simpler, higher-DX provider is not anti-scale.
It is often the most scalable decision a team can make, because the first thing that
needs to scale is not your Kubernetes cluster.

<KeyTakeaway label="The real scale">
The first thing that has to scale is your team's ability to ship. Everything else is
downstream of that.
</KeyTakeaway>

So yes, respect AWS. Respect GCP. Respect Azure. (Maybe not as much.) But do not worship
them.
